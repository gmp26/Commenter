A quick guide to markdown

Copy and paste these examples into the edit box to try them.

Simple text with line breaks


Here is some simple text
with a line break.

Leave a blank line to start a paragraph.
Spaces    are  collapsed into   one.

Add emphasis with *one*, **two**, or ***three*** asterisks.
Alternatively,
add emphasis with _one_, __two__, or ___three___ underscores.

Insert hyperlinks and images


[link to NRICH](http://nrich.maths.org)

![A small image](/favicon.ico)

Include mathematics using LaTeX

LaTeX maths should be bracketed by single $ signs if it is to appear inline, or by doubled $$ signs to appear centered and separate. The math stackexchange site has a basic tutorial and quick reference. 

Straight lines have equations of the form $y=ax+b$, 
but other equivalent forms are quite often very useful. 

For example, $$ \frac{y-3}{x-2} = 6 $$ will give
you a line of gradient $6$ that goes through 
the point $(2,3)$.

Add headers and sub-headers

Either underline h1 and h2 headers


Header 1
========

Header 2
--------

or add hashes up to h3.


# Header 1
## Header 2
### Header 3

Lists

Ordered, without paragraphs:


5.  Use any numbers you like
1.  and the list will be ordered
3.  as written but numbered from 1.

Unordered, with paragraphs:


*   A list item.

    With multiple paragraphs.

    *   And
    *   a nested list

Blockquotes


> Email-style angle brackets
> are used for blockquotes.

>> And, they can be nested.

> #### Headers work in blockquotes
 
> * You can quote a list.
> * and add __emphasis__.

Code Spans

<code> spans are delimited by backticks.

You can include literal backticks like `this` .

Horizontal Rules

Three or asterisks:

* * *

Manual Line Breaks

End a line with two or more spaces:

Roses are red,
Violets are blue.
